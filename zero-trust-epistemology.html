AI for Science Epistemic Hygiene
 

Zero-Trust Epistemology

In modern AI-for-science, the core challenge is no longer model capacity but epistemic hygiene. We debate feature engineering versus representation learning, intervention versus restraint, priors versus pretraining — but underneath, the same question repeats: what deserves trust, and when?

This essay reframes that question using a principle borrowed from cybersecurity and applied to scientific modeling: zero-trust epistemology. Not as skepticism, but as a discipline for building systems — human or artificial — that remain robust under uncertainty, distribution shift, and adversarial reality.

Zero-trust epistemology starts from a simple premise: assume every claim is compromised until proven otherwise.

The age of inherited certainty is over. No authority, no institution, no dataset gets root access to your belief system by default. Every statement is an unsigned packet traversing a hostile network.

In a zero-trust world, credibility is no longer a property of the source. It’s an emergent property of verification paths. You don’t “believe X because expert Y said so.” You believe X, provisionally, because independent routes of evidence converge under adversarial scrutiny.

Knowledge becomes a security architecture:

  • Segmentation: Keep domains of belief compartmentalized. A failure in one area doesn’t auto-propagate to the rest of your worldview.
  • Multi-factor justification: One argument is never enough; require orthogonal lines of support.
  • Continuous monitoring: Beliefs are not set-and-forget; they’re subject to live patching as new vulnerabilities (counterevidence) are discovered.

Under zero-trust epistemology, the self is not a passive consumer of “facts” but an active verification engine. You don’t outsource reality to a single provider. You run your own node. You cross-check, replay the logs, simulate alternative models, and watch what breaks.

This is not cynicism; it’s resilience. The point is not to trust nothing forever. The point is to trust only what has survived your best attempt to kill it — and to keep that kill-switch within reach.
