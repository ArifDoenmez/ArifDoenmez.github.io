The Bayesian Paradigm as a Duality Principle

Compared to forward probabilistic modeling, statistical analysis is an inversion problem: we want to retrieve “causes” (parameters) from “effects” (observations). Bayesian statistics makes this inversion explicit by turning likelihood into updated belief.

1) Forward modeling vs. inverse inference

A probabilistic model describes how future observations behave conditional on a parameter \(\theta\). In symbols, the model specifies a family \(f(x\mid\theta)\): given \(\theta\), it tells you what kinds of data \(x\) you should expect.

Statistical inference flips the direction: after observing \(x\), we want a summary about \(\theta\). This is the inversion purpose of statistics: learn about the generating mechanism from its outcomes.

2) Likelihood is “the same density in the opposite role”

Formally, the likelihood function is just the sampling density viewed as a function of \(\theta\) with \(x\) held fixed:

\[ \ell(\theta\mid x) = f(x\mid\theta). \]

Same formula, different interpretation: \(f(x\mid\theta)\) describes how data vary when \(\theta\) is known; \(\ell(\theta\mid x)\) ranks which \(\theta\) values are more compatible with the observed \(x\).

3) The inversion of probabilities: Bayes’ theorem (events)

For events \(A\) (cause) and \(E\) (effect) with \(P(E)\neq 0\), Bayes’ theorem relates \(P(A\mid E)\) and \(P(E\mid A)\):

\[ P(A\mid E)=\frac{P(E\mid A)P(A)}{P(E)}. \]

Interpretation: evidence \(E\) updates the plausibility of causes \(A\). If two causes are equally plausible a priori (\(P(A)=P(B)\)), then the posterior odds equal the likelihood ratio:

\[ \frac{P(A\mid E)}{P(B\mid E)}=\frac{P(E\mid A)}{P(E\mid B)}. \]

4) From events to parameters: priors and posteriors

Bayes and Laplace went further: uncertainty about the parameter \(\theta\) itself can be represented by a probability distribution \(\pi(\theta)\), the prior. After observing \(x\), this becomes the posterior \(\pi(\theta\mid x)\).

In continuous form, the update is:

\[ \pi(\theta\mid x) = \frac{f(x\mid\theta)\,\pi(\theta)}{\int f(x\mid\theta)\,\pi(\theta)\,d\theta}. \]

The denominator is the normalizing constant (the “evidence”): \[ m(x)=\int f(x\mid\theta)\pi(\theta)\,d\theta, \] ensuring the posterior integrates to 1.

5) Definition: Bayesian statistical model

A Bayesian statistical model is a parametric statistical model \(f(x\mid\theta)\) together with a prior distribution \(\pi(\theta)\) on the parameters.

6) The conceptual shift: unknown vs. random parameter

The revolutionary move is to treat \(\theta\) not only as unknown, but as uncertain in a way we can summarize probabilistically. This does not have to mean that \(\theta\) is literally produced by a physical random experiment. The prior is better read as a disciplined summary of available information (or lack of information) about \(\theta\), carried into the decision process.

Key takeaways:

  • Forward: \(f(x\mid\theta)\) predicts data given parameters.
  • Inverse: inference summarizes \(\theta\) given observed \(x\).
  • Likelihood: \(\ell(\theta\mid x)=f(x\mid\theta)\) (same expression, reversed role).
  • Bayes: posterior \(\propto\) likelihood \(\times\) prior.
  • Model: Bayesian model = \(f(x\mid\theta)\) + \(\pi(\theta)\).
